Affordable Housing

Introduction

A general rule for 'Affordable' housing, otherwise known as 'Social' Housing is a cost that does not exceed 30% of household gross income - the cost including local taxes, energy costs and water services. Not an easy thing to work out.

A previous book 'Job file 1960 - 1992' lists 176 building jobs carried out by architects Philip Cocker and Partners and 66 abortive designs. Of the built work 64 are of multiple house contracts of which most fall into the category currently known as 'affordable' housing. This earlier book of 2003 is mainly photographs with just basic information - site, date, client and contract value and a few layout drawings. The new internet availability of satellite maps and three dimensional aerial photography, together with the probability of much of the immediate future U.K. housing construction having to be fairly basic, inspire this retrospective look at what one architectural practice did during a previous period of financial stringency.

The satellite photography is of varying quality and has been selected as being the best offered by the two main providers. Sometimes the is no coverage at all. Drawn layouts provide a mere detailed image of a group of houses, but the big advantage of the sat' pics is that they show the current state on the ground now after up to over 40 years of use and also shows particularly well how the green landscape has fared and whether the provision of car parking is working.

All this may only be of passing interest to the general public, despite the vast media fuelled interest in their own and prospective homes, but when design work resumes with new non 'Luxury Executive' budgets , a few pointers may be gleaned by the professionals from these examples from the past. At least it is logging of a particular part of housing history.

Housing Statistics

The latest figures for the U.K. indicate a population of British born nationals of 53.9 million plus foreign born nationals of 6.5 million - total 60.4 million. The number of houses is about 25 million. On a replacement requirement of 100 years that is 250,000 new houses per year. Additional new houses are constantly demanded in normal times at perhaps the same number, so the new build figure of 500,000 houses per year is frequently mentioned. There have never been more than 300,000 build in the U.K. (in the 50's) so there is really a problem which can never be solved, unless the 800,000 owners of overseas houses moved abroad. An interesting idea.

On the other hand there is a big percentage of holiday houses in some areas which are only 90% occupied and thousands of empty blighted early Victorian streets in northern English towns controversially awaiting redevelopment.

The other question is whether the English population will continue to rise because of immigration and natural growth or not. Natural growth in major European countries such as France, Italy and Spain has already slowed as it has in Japan and of course China has struggled to restrict growth for some years now.

In Scotland the population is static at about 5 million, immigration is modest so the requirement is for replacement and some perceived new build. With a stock of 2.3 million houses the replacement requirement would be 23,000 per annum. Over the past few years the new build has been about 25k per year, so where is the problem? Shelter Scotland insist there are 200,000 on the various council waiting lists. The situation is confused, particularly when the E.U. tell us the U.K. population is header for 71m in 2030.

Housing Standards

In Scotland, the industrial revolution of the 19th century and consequent population explosion increased demand for shelter much beyond the supply. The Glasgow authorities reacted with the 'Ticketing' of tenement rooms with the maximum sleeping body count allowed, based on 300 cubic feet per person. Just enough space to sleep, have a personal box and turn around in. Do not ask about night soil. These measures were followed by the building of 'Model Lodging Houses' and 'Model Family Tenements' to demonstrate more decent standards. These tenements in the 1860's were restricted to four storys with 'single ends' (one room) and two room flats, each hroom having a fireplace and generally a recess for a 4' x 6' 'Scotch Double' bed. A communical water closet could be found off the stais half landing with an outside wall. Back courts were as little as 55ft. wide and front streets about 50ft. minimum. These distances were to allow fresh air to circulate the streets as it was hoped that this would help to reduce the many epidemics of the diseases that swept through towns as that time. The rebuilding of Paris with wide streets was to help the army to keep down the revolting lower classes - a different problem. Good clean water supplies and good drainage was in fact the way to healthy licing and this work proceeded along with the new tenements.

In Edibuburgh, tenements had been built in the Old Town to a massive 15 storeys in parts, but occasional collapses and more frequent fire disasters curbed the appetite for height and with the development of the New Town a maximum of four storeys was the norm. In general the New Town houses were ar from 'affordable' but some more modest tenements were built, kind of out of sight, but as the privileged moved out of the Old Town, other lesser mortals moved in.

Progress with the provision of tenement property was slow but steady. In Glasgow, Edinburgh and the other Scottish cities and towns, despite setbacks like several Great and lesser wars and the collapse of The City of Glasgow Bank in 1878 which ruined most of the builders for decased and interesetingly put the directors in jail.

A major shift occurred after the 1914/18 war when 'Homes fit for Heroes' was the cry, and Garden Cities were the new answer. Tenement flats had progresses and the best had full bathrooms and seperate kitchens and some flats for the better off were quite large. New developments in many areas were based on Garden City principles and Council built streets included semi-detatched two story houses with all mod cons and floor areas of around 1100 sq. ft. Private developers were also busy building large areas of detached bungalows of similar floor areas. This type of building took up more land of course which at the time was probably not expensive. During WW2 house building ceased and in 1945 there was the old shortage problem again. The Labour government concentrated all avaoilable resources on council house building after giving exports top priority. This took the form of two storey terraced housing, some 3 and 4 storey flats and a large number of multi-story flats in the major cities. The low rise building was by and large fit for purpose, if often uninspired, but the high rise programme, much of it built with new and untested systems, were expensive, and without property management was often a failure. The high rises were also subsidised much more than other development, an anomoly which was justified by the need to create space for new city roads and redevelopment wriggle room.

House areas during this period started off with the Bevan ideal of 1000 sq.ft for a family house, but this was reduced to 850 sq.ft in the early 50's to hit P.M. McMillan's 300,000 house target. This was lifted back to 1000 in the 60's after the 'Parker Morris' Report. The 80's brought new Building Regulations which modified area standards so that 850 again would do.

In the 90's the accent was on 'Executive Luxury' development and 'Social' and 'Affordable' only applied to a minority of houses and flats'. During all of this yo-yo-ing the modest standard of 9sq.m per person was the rule for all the old Soviety Block except for the 'Nomenclatura' privileged classes, and probably still applies even though Russia has a reducing population. By contract it has been said that new build houses in the Netherlands are 50% larger than in the U.K. There there is the internationally respected Japanese mathematics Professor who has lived for many years in a 9 sq.m room. He eats out of course but loves it.

Quality

Reference has been made to 'Indifferent' low rise design and the failures of high rises. Why is this so? A recent report by the English government watchdog C.A.B.E. (Commision for Architecture and the Built Environment) says that "we have douns that just 18% of new social homes meet a good or very good standard. This means that the design of four fifths of homes is average, poor or very poor. The findings echo those of a study of private housing conducted by C.A.B.E. between 2001 and 2007 which found that exactly the same number were average, poor or very poor."

What boxes are C.A.B.E. ticking? It used to be 'Shelter, access and services, form, colour and texture' plus green stuff if appropriate. It was always going to be hard to tick all the boxes with say hundreds of two storey terraced houses with catalogue windows and porridge like roughcaste walls. History shows that quality is a rare commodity and will probably stay so.

Densities

Some recent studies have concluded that compact urban settlements are the most efficient by all criteria. The natural evolvement of existing towns and cities confirms this view. Building at low densities is not thereforse a reasonable option for large increments of housing building, so the choice ranges between the most popular familiy densities of 5/7 d.p.a. and a more uban density of 25/30 d.p.a. plus a small proportion at higher densities in the larget cities. In normal times and given a choice families will migrate to suit their pocket and choice. Again this is what has happened in free markets. A recent 'Mori' poll indicates that 95% of people want to live in a house and only 3% prefer a flat, a result which is not surprising but is hardly likely to be realised. One aspect of medium densities which has been somewhat unremarked, is the benefit of an improved microclimate in two storey courtyards and gardens. Aerial photographs demonstrate how very urban and dense seeming front streets hide luxuriant back gardens and how valuable they are. Another interesting factor in cities is how oftern in a couple of centuries certain areas have been redeveloped over and over again. Think of Edinburgh High street or Glasgow Govan. It is never impossible to redevelop an area, hopefully better than before.

Procurement

Over the three centuries of serious housebuilding in the U.K. development has been achieved by various agencies, Industrial companies - Bournville, New Lanark etc landowners and builders - City trusts - local Town and District Councils and Housing Associations of various types. In terms of quantity and speed the largest cities in the post W.W.2. push build several thousands each for many years to good effect. In Scotland the Government agency S.S.H.A. (Scottish Special Housing Agency) assisted by building 110,000 houses between 1940 and 1985. Tellingly it is apopcryphal that not a single S.S.H.A site was started with complete legal possession.

In recent years much affordable housing has been achieved only because developers have been required to provide a proportion of it in thier housing developments. When the market recovers from the current problems this may not be possible, or maybe for a while most housing will have to be 'Affordable' for economic reasons. In fact a wide range of providers is always going to be advisable.

In considering 'Affordability' history indicates that some subsidy is required at some point in the delivery chain and will be so for a long time. How to do this is something the politicians will argue about forever.

Prognosis

'The art of foretelling the progress of a disease.' This definition by Collins is very appropriate if you are on the side of the nimbys. The developments illustrated here were fairly typical of the time when they were built. The noble intention was to provide families with comfortable houses in a pleasant environment and to provider maximum density consistent with proper amenity, but of course the world is paved with good intentions. Some csiriticisms are noted in the brief commentaries by an involved but remote observer. Remote because of the need to get on with the next job with only very limited return visits to completed work. It is frustrating as an arhictect churning out designes year after year, quite happily of course - but however close to clients, there is very little feedback from the owners or tenants on the important subject of life in our houses. Technical, professional and lay magazines occassionally have useful feedback content but no recent academic studies seem to have been made and disseminated. Maybe there is if one lookes, byt one problem is that most architects arrogantly feel that they know exactly what is required, but the C.A.B.E comments suggests that many designers are actually floundering about. Looking at some recent developments of tightly packed lacklistre four storey flats and non stop boxes of detacthed 'villas', one wonders if artchitects were employed at all. It is stil quite feasible for a good draughtsman to knock up a set of drawings for a large scheme of repetitive housing.

The planning system has not been discussed in this study, perhaps because the subject is too big for here and curiously at the end of the day has only a minimal effect on things but seems to have been unable to produce consistently good results. The Planning system is probably as good as it might get but Planning staff could probably assume more 'can do' rather than 'no can do' attitudes. Easy to say and the tripartite interests of developers, planning staff and elected members are likely to persist permanently, not to mention the poll that tells us that 85% of the population object to the development of everything, anywhere.

A penultimate note on a neglected but perennial subject - prefabs. Prefabrication has developed much since the quite succesfful post W.W.2. prefab programme and perhaps it should be tried again for the lower densities. Not please with imports, there is much experienced expertise in the U.K. parricularly Scotland. Allow a generous budget for green landscape and a quality tiled roof. The public will take to them.

A final note in this section is about sheltered housing which must be a growth industry. This can take many forms and have various lifestyle specifications but all types will assist in using the housing stock as efficiently as possible.

End Note

Towards the end of the last squeeze we were pushing the envelope in two directions. One downwards in that the minimal spec. of a useful flat should be two bed flat - one double and one single. This was much more useful than a one bed two person flat, as the sinfle room could be used with a double bund bed for small children for perhaps a year or so. This could be achieved in a D.o.E. book area of 46 sq.m for a two person flat.

The other direction was upwards, in that some 3 bed flats were given two bathrooms - a move towards more comfortable living.

Who knows what economies will have to be made or if any, when the slump lifts. in the 50's one authority left off the bath panel to save 10s. (50p now) and another left the interior painting to tenants. As late as 1975 a major houses builder 'pioneered' a bed-sit flat of about 28 sq.m. which was much featured nationally, but quietly dropped within a short time. Probably the market did not like it. The housing problems of the U.K. and Europe pale by comparison with the developing rest of the world, and the only answer to this is only just creeping back on to the agenda.

Philip Cocker, March 09.

Work

Grangemouth

Kersiebank Road, 210 Council Houses

The two storey terraces with some four storey blocks of flats, are arranged in linked terraces to form interesting pedestrian courts and walkways. Garaging is in rear vehicular courts, which makes for some long carries for furniture vans etc, but this is part of the compromise. The terraces are virtually single aspect with gardens some of which conveniently connect to a garage. The eaves level was kept low by having a solid ground floor - no steps required, and a 150mm coomb ceiling at roof level. The external walls were built with 'silver grey' concrete facing bricks, perhaps a first for public housing. It was said that Scottish brickies could not lay facings as they were only used to laying commons for roughcast. Not true and rather condescending.

The outer walls also had an insulating inner leaf block called 'Siporex'. This was in advance of it's time, went liquidate but is no newly produced by others. The ground floors had electric underfloor heading, though this was difficult to repair and electric block storage is probably now fitted. The running cost of this, hot water and machines is still only 2.2p per H.W.hour on some tarriffs. 150mm quilt insulation was also installed in the roofs. The insulation levels were about 30 years in front of the legistration . Catalogue kitchens were not then available, so purpose built units were fitted. The flat blocks had drying areas on the rood, now replaced with pitched tiles. At that time the Scottish Development Department would not approve landscape costs other than grass, but a willing Parks Department and tenants managed to do what can be seen in the photographs.

These houses were built on the wartime Grangemouth airfield which operated between 1939 and 1945 when various Squadrons flew Spitfire and Hurricane fighters and Blenheim bombers. Secret testing of Mustarg Gas took place using Lysander aircraft and many Polish aircrew were also trained here. Sometimes you do not know the half of it!
